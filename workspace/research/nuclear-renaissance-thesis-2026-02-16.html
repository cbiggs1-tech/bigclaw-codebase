1. Executive Summary & Investment Thesis

Why the Nuclear Renaissance Is Real

The convergence of three megatrends has created the most favorable environment for nuclear energy since the Atoms for Peace era:

1. AI Data Center Power Demand — The Insatiable Appetite Big Tech's combined AI infrastructure spend is projected at $650 billion in 2026 (Amazon, Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft). Each hyperscale data center demands 500MW–2GW of reliable, 24/7 baseload power — exactly what nuclear provides and what renewables alone cannot. Meta alone just signed deals for 6.6 GW of nuclear capacity across TerraPower, Oklo, and Vistra. Microsoft's 20-year PPA with Constellation to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 (now "Crane Clean Energy Center") signals that Big Tech is willing to underwrite nuclear economics directly.

2. Policy Tailwinds — Executive Order 14300 & NRC Reform President Trump's EO 14300 mandates comprehensive NRC reform with fixed licensing deadlines: 18 months maximum for new-reactor construction-and-operation applications, 12 months for license renewals. The NRC has launched a major reorganization to comply, including new offices and streamlined review processes. A proposed microreactor licensing rule is targeted for March 2026, with a final rule by September 2026. The ADVANCE Act (bipartisan) further accelerates advanced reactor deployment. This is the most pro-nuclear regulatory environment in decades.

3. Energy Security & Grid Reliability Russian enrichment dependence (~40% of global SWU capacity) has made domestic nuclear fuel a national security imperative. Grid reliability concerns — from Texas winter storms to California rolling blackouts — have rehabilitated nuclear's reputation as the ultimate baseload source. The conversation has shifted from "how do we shut them down?" to "how fast can we build them?"

The Investment Thesis in One Sentence

Nuclear is the only energy source that satisfies Big Tech's simultaneous demands for massive baseload capacity, 24/7 reliability, and carbon-free credentials — and the policy environment has never been more supportive.

The Catch

9. Ten Domain Expert Questions

These are questions a 43-year nuclear veteran would ask that Wall Street analysts wouldn't think of. Use these to evaluate these companies with your unique expertise.

1. Comanche Peak License Renewal Readiness

"What's the status of the Comanche Peak aging management program for the reactor vessel, containment tendons, and underground piping? Are there any known degradation issues that could complicate license renewal?"

Why it matters: License renewal (beyond 60 years to 80 years under Subsequent License Renewal) is not automatic. The NRC requires comprehensive aging management reviews. You've seen 43 years of the plant's history. You know where the bodies are buried — literally, what components have degraded, what repairs were deferred, what inspection findings were dispositioned. This is the kind of insight that determines whether VST's license renewal is a rubber stamp or a fight.

2. NuScale's Natural Circulation Claims

"NuScale claims their SMR uses natural circulation to eliminate coolant pumps and associated loss-of-coolant scenarios. Based on your understanding of PWR thermal-hydraulics, at what power level and containment configuration does natural circulation become unreliable? Have they adequately addressed boron dilution transients?"

Why it matters: Natural circulation is elegant but has physical limits. In large PWRs, forced circulation is necessary because natural circulation can't move enough coolant at full power. NuScale's 77 MWe size is small enough that natural circulation should work — but the NRC review of their safety analysis would reveal whether all transient scenarios (including boron dilution, which causes reactivity insertion) were adequately analyzed.

3. OKLO's NRC Denial — The Real Problem

"OKLO's 2022 combined license application was denied for insufficient safety analysis. As someone who's been through NRC licensing reviews, what does a denial at that stage tell you about the company's nuclear engineering depth? Is the deficiency fixable, or does it suggest fundamental gaps?"

Why it matters: Getting denied by the NRC isn't like failing a test you can retake. It means the staff reviewed your application and found it so deficient that they couldn't even begin the detailed technical review. This suggests either the company didn't have enough experienced nuclear engineers, or they underestimated what the NRC requires. Both are concerning for a company promising commercial reactors by 2030.
